What Is Vodka?

Vodka is a distilled beverage composed primarily of water and ethanol. Traditionally made by distilling fermented grains or potatoes, though modern versions can be made from fruits or even sugar.

Origins & History

Vodka’s name comes from the Slavic word “voda”, meaning "little water". It is believed to have originated in Eastern Europe, particularly Russia or Poland, as early as the 9th century, with commercial production ramping up in the 14th–15th century. Originally used as a medicine and antiseptic.

Production Facts

Distilled multiple times for purity; some vodkas are distilled up to 7 times. Most vodka is filtered through charcoal or quartz sand to remove impurities. It’s usually bottled at 40% alcohol by volume (ABV), though this can vary.
