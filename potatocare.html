Potato Care

What are they? Seed potatoes are small tubers (not actual seeds) that are used to grow new potato plants. They should be certified disease-free. Fun Fact: Each eye on a potato is a potential new plant! Pre-Sprouting (Chitting) Place seed potatoes in egg cartons or trays, eyes up, in a cool, bright place (like a windowsill) for 2-4 weeks. This helps them develop strong sprouts before planting — leading to earlier and stronger growth.

Best Time to Plant: 2-4 weeks before the last expected frost. Soil Requirements: Loose, well-draining soil (sandy loam is ideal) pH between 5.0–6.5 Enriched with compost but low in nitrogen (to avoid too much foliage growth) Spacing: Space rows 2.5–3 feet apart. Place seed potatoes 12 inches apart, 4 inches deep, with sprouts facing up. Hilling Method: Cover lightly at first. As they grow, keep mounding soil around stems to prevent sunlight from reaching the tubers (which causes greening and solanine – toxic!).

Watering: Potatoes need 1–1.5 inches of water per week. Critical times: when tubers are forming (usually 4-6 weeks after planting). Avoid overhead watering — use drip irrigation to prevent leaf diseases. Fertilization: Use a balanced fertilizer (5-10-10 NPK). Too much nitrogen = leafy plants and small tubers.

Factor Optimal Conditions
Sunlight Full sun — at least 6 hours/day
Soil Temp 45–75°F — Ideal for sprouting and root development
Air Temp 60–70°F — Best for overall plant growth and tuber formation
Heat Issues Temps above 80°F may slow tuber growth and increase risk of scab or poor yields

Common Pests: Colorado Potato Beetle: Hand-pick and destroy eggs/larvae. Use neem oil or insecticidal soap. Wireworms & Cutworms: Avoid planting where sod has recently been turned. Aphids: Transmit viruses. Encourage ladybugs or spray with soapy water. Common Diseases: Late Blight (Phytophthora infestans): Same one that caused the Irish Potato Famine! Appears as dark, oily spots on leaves and stems. Prevent with copper-based fungicides and resistant varieties. Scab (Streptomyces): Caused by alkaline soil. Keep pH below 6.0. Blackleg / Soft rot: Avoid overly wet soils and handle tubers gently.

Early Harvest: 10–12 weeks after planting for new potatoes (small and tender). Main Harvest: When foliage turns yellow and dies back (~15–20 weeks). Cure the Potatoes: Let them sit in a dark, well-ventilated area at 50–60°F for 10–14 days. This toughens skin and improves storage life.

Ideal Conditions: Cool (40–50°F), dark, and humid (85–90% humidity). Never store in the fridge (starch converts to sugar). Check Regularly: Remove any sprouting or soft potatoes to avoid spreading spoilage. --- Extra Tips & Curiosities Potato Towers & Grow Bags: Great for small spaces! Keep adding soil as the plants grow. Companion Plants: Potatoes grow well with beans, corn, and cabbage. Avoid tomatoes and sunflowers (can share diseases). Rotation: Don’t plant potatoes in the same spot for at least 2–3 years. Rotate with legumes or leafy greens.
